Ingredients/Equipment Needed

Equipment Needed

  • Kitchen Scale (Set it to measure in grams.)
  • A Good Mixer (You'll need something with enough power to mix bread dough, such as a KitchenAid.)
  • Cooking Thermometer (I use a thermometer gun, but anything that can give you a quick reading will do.)
  • Parchment Paper
  • Lame Knife (Pronounced like "Lom", this knife is specifically used for scoring bread dough. Alternatively you can use a razor blade.)
  • Cooling Rack(s) (If you don't have any cooling racks, use anything that will elevate the finished bread loaves off the ground and allow air underneath them.

Ingredients for 1 Loaf

  • 300g- 90°F Water
  • 100g- Ripe Starter
  • 400g- Bread Flour
  • 100g- Whole Wheat Flour (I use whole grain wheat berries and grind them to flour in my blender. You'll need a high powered blender to do this.)
  • 10g- Sea Salt

Ingredients for 2 Loaves

  • 600g- 90°F Water
  • 200g- Ripe Starter
  • 800g- Bread Flour
  • 200g- Whole Wheat Flour (or other flour of choice)
  • 20g- Sea Salt

Instructions

Making Sourdough Bread requires planning and time. Because you're not using traditional yeasts, rising time takes significantly more time, plust the extra time allows for the bread to proof and soak in the sour flavor.

It is recommended to feed your starter the night before around 7:30pm, and then let it sit overnight. You'll start making the bread dough the following morning. For instructions on feeding your starter, click HERE.

*Note: This recipe uses whole grain flour which I make from grinding whole grain wheat berries. If using all-purpose flour, you will be using different amounts of flour than what will be shown. It's recommended to do some research on other sourdough recipes using the preferred flour of your choice. Once you have the basic dough recipe, you can use the rest of the instructions here.

  1. Check the starter that you fed the night before. You'll know it's ready when you can see that it has grown and it has air pockets throughout from the expanding gasses.

  2. Measure out 300g of 90°F Water (600g for two loaves), and add to a large mixing bowl. The warm water will create an environment for the bacteria to feed and make your dough rise. (I use a thermometer gun as shown in the picture. But you can use anything that will give you a quick temperature reading.) (You also will want to place your mixing bowl on top of a kitchen scale and tare out the weight. You'll tare it out after adding each ingredient.)

  3. Measure out 100g of your Ripe Starter (200g for two loaves), and add to the warm water. Mix together until combined.

  4. Tare your scale again, and measure 400g of Bread Flour (800g for two loaves).

  5. Tare your scale again, and measure 100g of Whole Wheat Flour (200g for two loaves).

  6. Tare your scale again, and measure 10g of Sea Salt (20g for two loaves).

  7. Combine all ingredients by hand or use a mixer.

    *Note: If using a mixer, then mix on the LOWEST SETTING for no more than 3 minutes using a dough hook. The dough is very fragile and mixing it aggressivley can keep it from rising and give you flat bread with no air pockets.

  8. When the dough has been sufficiently combined, cover the bowl with plastic wrap and let the dough "rest" for 30 minutes. This allows the gluten/gliadin to relax and easily reform itself into the long protein chains which are the superstructure of the finished loaf.

  9. After the 30 minutes, Remove the dough from the bowl and place it on a lightly floured surface.

  10. Gently mead it using the "Stretch and Fold" technique. You can view an example HERE. I usually fold it once for each side.

    Be gentle with the dough and don't knead it as you would normal bread loaves as this can also damage your dough's ability to rise and produce airpockets.

  11. After stretching and folding, gently shape the dough into a ball again by tucking the open, folded ends underneath. Then place the dough back in the bowl and cover with plastic wrap. Leave for 1 Hour.

  12. After the 1 Hour, repeat this process 2 more times for an additional 2 Hours.

  13. Remove the dough from the bowl.

    If making two loaves of bread, divide the dough into two equal sized amounts.

  14. Prepare a banneton basket (or two if making two loaves) by coating the inside with flour. Place the dough ball(s) inside, and cover again with plastic wrap.

    *Note: I strongly recommed getting banneton baskets as they give your finished bread a beautiful crust with decorative flour lines. If you don't have banneton baskets, you can alternatively use regular bowls lined with a lint-free cloth or towel. You will still want to dust the cloth with flour.

    Leave bowl(s) on counter for 3 hours at room temperature.

    After the 3 hours, place the basket(s) in the refrigerator to proof for 12-18 hours or overnight.

    *Note: You can experiment with how sour your dough turns out by how long you proof your dough. The longer you wait, the more sour your dough. The 12-18 hours produces a milder sour flavor, but you can go multiple days for proofing your dough.

  15. After proofing, remove your baskets from the fridge. Don't remove the dough from the basket yet.

    Preheat your oven to it's highest setting or a maximum of 500°F.

  16. While your oven is preheating, place a cookie sheet on the lower rack to preheat with it. Boil a pan full of water. Once your oven is preheated, then pour the water onto the hot cookie sheet pan in the oven. WARNING: This will instantly generate a fair amount of steam and hot water may splash.

    The purpose of this step is to create a humid environment for your dough. This creates the hard outer crust on the finished bread.

  17. Prepare one (or two) cookie sheet with a sheet of parchment paper on each. Then GENTLY turn over the banneton basket over the parchment paper.

    *Note: Your dough is extremely fragile right now, and it may stick to the basket. You can gently bend the baskets to help the dough fall out, but ultimately you'll need to let gravity do it's thing. The dough is full of air and can very easily go flat if you're too rough with it.

  18. When ready to put the dough in the oven, take a lame knife or a razor blade and score the dough. (I like to score in an "X" shape. Scoring your bread will start to make it go flat, so don't do this step until you're ready to immediately put it in the oven.

    *Note: A lame knife is an extremely sharp fine blade used for scoring bread. This is the best way to score bread dough. If you do not have a lame knife, then use a razor blade. Normal kitchen knives are usually not fine and sharp enough to do this without pulling your dough where you're cutting.

  19. Quickly open your oven and place the sheets inside to prevent as much moisture from escaping as you can.

    Bake for 10 Minutes.

  20. After the 10 Minutes, remove the cookie sheet with water in it from the oven.

    Continue baking for another 20-30 minutes.

    *Note: Every oven is different as far as how long to bake your bread. 20 Minutes is perfect for me and any longer will burn the bread.

  21. After baking, remove from the oven.

  22. Immediately place the bread loaves on cooling racks. Leave for at least 1 hour.

    Enjoy!

    *IMPORTANT!: As tempting as it will be to cut into the bread and have a fresh warm slice, DO NOT cut into it! Letting it cool for at least an hour allows the bread to rid itself of excess moisture that makes the bread gummy. This is also why you should use cooling racks instead of leaving on the pan or on a counter top.
